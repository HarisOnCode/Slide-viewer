Design Terminology

Glossary: Quick Reference

138

Chiton

Greek

A simple tunic garment of lighter linen that was worn by both genders and all ages. It consisted of a wide, rectangular tube of material secured along the shoulders and upper arms by a series of fasteners.

Himation

Greek

A mantle or wrap worn by ancient Greek men and women over the chiton. It was essentially a large rectangular piece of heavy fabric, usually wool, draped over the left shoulder and about the body.

Stola

Roman

The traditional garment of Roman women, corresponding to the toga worn by men. It was a long, pleated dress worn over an undertunic (tunica intima) and was the symbol of a married woman's status.

Usekh

Egyptian

A broad collar or necklace worn by the elite of ancient Egypt. Made of rows of beads, gold, or faience, it covered the shoulders and chest, serving both decorative and protective (amuletic) functions.

Paniers

Rococo

Wide side-hoops worn under skirts to extend the width of the hips while leaving the front and back flat. This silhouette created a broad canvas for displaying woven patterns and embroidery.

Whiplash

Art Nouveau

A decorative motif characterized by asymmetrical, sinuous lines that curve back on themselves, resembling a cracking whip. It symbolizes the organic energy and movement central to the style.

Ziggurat

Art Deco

A stepped pyramid structure originating from ancient Mesopotamia, adopted as a key motif in Art Deco architecture and design. It represents the style's geometric, skyscraper-inspired aesthetic.

Streamline

Art Deco

A later Art Deco style (Streamline Moderne) emphasizing aerodynamic lines, rounded corners, and horizontal bands. Influenced by speed and motion in transportation design.
